# NDIS Scheme Cost Growth and Fiscal Risk

## Overview
This project analyses the medium-term fiscal sustainability of the Australian National Disability Insurance Scheme (NDIS). Using demographic, participation, and cost modelling, the report examines how participant growth, average plan costs, and policy reforms interact to drive expenditure under alternative scenarios.
The analysis focuses on identifying the primary drivers of cost growth and assessing the relative effectiveness of eligibility reforms versus cost containment measures.

## Tools
* R for data cleaning, data wrangling, exploratory data analysis (EDA), scenario modelling, and data visualisation for policy‑oriented reporting.
* Microsoft 365 / document reporting tools

## Key Questions Addressed
* What are the primary contributors to NDIS cost growth over the medium term?
* How sensitive is total expenditure to changes in participant numbers versus average plan costs?
* How do eligibility tightening reforms affect projected costs?
* What fiscal risks arise under alternative economic and demographic scenarios?

## Data and Methodology:
Combines a brief EDA with forward projections of:
* Participant growth
* Annualised pan budgets
* Participant payments/utilisation

Scenario modelling is used to compare:
* Status quo (baseline models) scenario
* Adverse economic and demographic scenario
* Eligibility reform scenario targeting specific demographic cohorts

Results are communicated primarily through visualisation and non-technical interpretation.

## Key Findings
* Total NDIS expenditure growth is driven more strongly by increases in average plan costs than by participant growth.
* The likelihood of extreme annual budget increases poses a material risk to long‑term sustainability.
* Younger participants (under age 10) account for a growing share of total plan costs under baseline assumptions.
* Eligibility tightening targeted at the under-10 age cohort showed minimal potential to reduce projected expenditure in the medium-term materially.

## Policy Implications
* Cost‑containment measures targeting average plan growth may be more effective for sustainability than eligibility changes alone.
* Reforms focused on early‑age cohorts could have outsized long‑term fiscal effects rather than medium-term.
* Increasing utilisation rates amplify fiscal risks even when budget caps appear controlled.
